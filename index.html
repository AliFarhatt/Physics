Foundation

Physical Quantities and Units

Key idea

A physical quantity is a measurable property. A number without a unit is usually incomplete in physics.

Examples

  • Distance: measured in meters, m.
  • Time: measured in seconds, s.
  • Mass: measured in kilograms, kg.
  • Force: measured in newtons, N.

Important habit

Before solving, convert all quantities to SI units unless the question clearly allows another unit.


Examples: \(1\,\text{km}=1000\,\text{m}\), \(1\,\text{h}=3600\,\text{s}\), \(1\,\text{mA}=10^{-3}\,\text{A}\).

Topic 1 - Mechanics

Kinematics: Describing Motion

Kinematics describes motion without asking what caused the motion. We study position, displacement, distance, speed, velocity, and acceleration.

Main question

Where is the object, how fast is it moving, and how is its motion changing?

Core tools

  • Formulas with time
  • Displacement-time graphs
  • Careful distinction between scalar and vector quantities
Kinematics - Idea 1

Distance and Displacement

Distance

The total length of the path traveled. It is a scalar: it has magnitude only.


Distance can never be negative.

Displacement

The straight-line change in position from start to finish. It is a vector: it has magnitude and direction.


Displacement can be positive, negative, or zero depending on direction.

Kinematics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Distance vs Displacement

Problem

A student walks 40 m east, then 15 m west. Find the total distance and the displacement.

1
Total distanceDistance counts the whole path: \(40+15=55\,\text{m}\).
2
Choose directionTake east as positive, west as negative.
3
Displacement\(\Delta x=+40-15=+25\,\text{m}\).
4
ConclusionDistance = \(55\,\text{m}\). Displacement = \(25\,\text{m east}\).

Common mistake: Do not subtract for distance. Distance ignores direction; displacement uses direction.

Kinematics - Idea 2

Speed and Velocity

Big distinction

Speed tells how fast an object moves. Velocity tells how fast and in which direction its position changes.

Average speed
\[v_{\text{speed}}=\frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}\]
Average velocity
\[\vec v_{\text{avg}}=\frac{\text{displacement}}{\text{total time}}=\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\]

Speed

Scalar. It is always non-negative.

Velocity

Vector. Its sign or direction matters.

Kinematics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Speed vs Velocity

Problem

A runner completes one full lap of a circular track of length 400 m in 80 s and ends where they started. Find average speed and average velocity.

1
DistanceTotal distance is one full lap: \(400\,\text{m}\).
2
DisplacementStart and finish are the same, so displacement is \(0\,\text{m}\).
3
Average speed\(v=\frac{400}{80}=5\,\text{m/s}\).
4
Average velocity\(\vec v=\frac{0}{80}=0\,\text{m/s}\).

This example proves speed and velocity are not the same thing.

Kinematics - Idea 3

Determining Speed from Distance and Time

When the motion is described by total distance and total time, use average speed.

Average speed
\[v=\frac{d}{t}\]

Find speed

\(v=\frac{d}{t}\)

Find distance

\(d=vt\)

Find time

\(t=\frac{d}{v}\)

Unit check: If distance is in meters and time is in seconds, speed is in m/s.

Kinematics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Determining Average Speed

Problem

A cyclist travels 1.8 km in 6 min. Find the average speed in m/s.

1
Convert distance\(1.8\,\text{km}=1800\,\text{m}\).
2
Convert time\(6\,\text{min}=360\,\text{s}\).
3
Apply formula\(v=\frac{d}{t}=\frac{1800}{360}\).
4
Answer\(v=5\,\text{m/s}\).
Kinematics - Idea 4

Acceleration

Definition

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with time. It tells how quickly velocity changes.

Average acceleration
\[a=\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}=\frac{v_f-v_i}{t}\]

Positive acceleration

Velocity becomes more positive with time.

Negative acceleration

Velocity becomes less positive, or more negative, with time.

Zero acceleration

Velocity is constant.

Kinematics - Idea 4

Acceleration Is Not Always “Speeding Up”

A very common mistake is thinking acceleration always means an object is getting faster. In physics, acceleration means velocity is changing.

Speeding up

If velocity and acceleration have the same direction, the speed increases.


Example: moving east with acceleration east.

Slowing down

If velocity and acceleration have opposite directions, the speed decreases.


Example: moving east with acceleration west.

Kinematics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Acceleration

Problem

A car increases its velocity from 6 m/s to 22 m/s in 8 s. Find its acceleration.

1
Known values\(v_i=6\,\text{m/s}\), \(v_f=22\,\text{m/s}\), \(t=8\,\text{s}\).
2
Formula\(a=\frac{v_f-v_i}{t}\).
3
Substitute\(a=\frac{22-6}{8}=\frac{16}{8}\).
4
Answer\(a=2\,\text{m/s}^2\).
Kinematics - Idea 5

Determining Final Velocity

If acceleration is constant, the acceleration formula can be rearranged to find final velocity.

From acceleration
\[a=\frac{v_f-v_i}{t}\quad \Rightarrow \quad v_f=v_i+at\]

Use this when

You know initial velocity, acceleration, and time.

Meaning

The change in velocity is \(at\). So final velocity equals initial velocity plus the change.

Kinematics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Determining Final Velocity

Problem

A cart starts at \(3\,\text{m/s}\) and accelerates at \(1.5\,\text{m/s}^2\) for \(6\,\text{s}\). Find its final velocity.

1
Known values\(v_i=3\,\text{m/s}\), \(a=1.5\,\text{m/s}^2\), \(t=6\,\text{s}\).
2
Formula\(v_f=v_i+at\).
3
Substitute\(v_f=3+(1.5)(6)\).
4
Answer\(v_f=12\,\text{m/s}\).
Kinematics - Idea 6

Displacement-Time Graphs

Main idea

On a displacement-time graph, the slope gives velocity.

Slope of x-t graph
\[\text{slope}=\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}=v\]
Kinematics - Idea 6

What the Shape of an x-t Graph Means

Horizontal line

Displacement is constant, so velocity is zero. The object is at rest.

Straight sloped line

Slope is constant, so velocity is constant.

Steeper line

A greater slope means greater speed.

Positive slope

Velocity is positive: motion in the positive direction.

Negative slope

Velocity is negative: motion in the opposite direction.

Kinematics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Velocity from a Graph

Problem

A displacement-time graph is a straight line passing through \((t,x)=(2\,\text{s},4\,\text{m})\) and \((8\,\text{s},22\,\text{m})\). Find the velocity.

1
Use slopeVelocity is the slope of the displacement-time graph.
2
Calculate changes\(\Delta x=22-4=18\,\text{m}\), \(\Delta t=8-2=6\,\text{s}\).
3
Divide\(v=\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}=\frac{18}{6}\).
4
Answer\(v=3\,\text{m/s}\).
Kinematics - Mini Quiz

Mini Quiz 1: Kinematics

Instructions

Solve without looking at the answers. Show formulas and units.

  1. A student walks 25 m north, then 10 m south. Find distance and displacement.
  2. A car travels 540 m in 45 s. Find average speed.
  3. A train changes velocity from 12 m/s to 30 m/s in 9 s. Find acceleration.
  4. A cyclist starts at 4 m/s and accelerates at 0.8 m/s² for 10 s. Find final velocity.
  5. On an x-t graph, two points are \((3\,\text{s}, 12\,\text{m})\) and \((7\,\text{s}, 0\,\text{m})\). Find the velocity and explain the sign.
Kinematics - Solutions

Mini Quiz 1: Solutions

  1. Distance \(=25+10=35\,\text{m}\). Displacement \(=25-10=15\,\text{m north}\).
  2. \(v=\frac{540}{45}=12\,\text{m/s}\).
  3. \(a=\frac{30-12}{9}=2\,\text{m/s}^2\).
  4. \(v_f=v_i+at=4+(0.8)(10)=12\,\text{m/s}\).
  5. \(v=\frac{0-12}{7-3}=\frac{-12}{4}=-3\,\text{m/s}\). The negative sign means motion is in the negative direction.
Topic 1 - Mechanics

Dynamics: Forces and Newton’s Second Law

Dynamics asks why motion changes. The answer is usually force: pushes, pulls, weight, tension, normal force, and other interactions.

Central idea

Forces are vectors. To find the effect on motion, first find the net force.

Main equation

\(\sum \vec F = m\vec a\)


Net force causes acceleration.

Dynamics - Idea 1

What Is a Force?

Definition

A force is a push or pull that can change the motion or shape of an object.

Vector quantity

A force has magnitude and direction.

Unit

The SI unit of force is the newton, written \(\text{N}\).

Effect

An unbalanced force produces acceleration.

Unit relation
\[1\,\text{N}=1\,\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}^2\]
Dynamics - Idea 2

Representing Forces Graphically

Forces are represented by arrows. The arrow gives both magnitude and direction.

Length

The longer the arrow, the larger the force.

Direction

The arrow points in the direction of the force.

Dynamics - Idea 2

Free-Body Diagrams

Definition

A free-body diagram is a simplified drawing showing only the object and the forces acting on it.

Rule: Do not draw forces that the object exerts on other objects. Draw only forces acting on the object.

Dynamics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Reading a Force Diagram

Problem

A 5 kg box is pushed to the right by 30 N and pulled to the left by 10 N. Represent the forces and find the net horizontal force.

1
Choose positive directionLet right be positive.
2
Write forces with signsRight force: \(+30\,\text{N}\). Left force: \(-10\,\text{N}\).
3
Add forces\(F_{\text{net}}=30-10=20\,\text{N}\).
4
DirectionThe net force is \(20\,\text{N}\) to the right.
Dynamics - Idea 3

Adding Two Force Vectors Along One Line

Same direction

Add magnitudes: \(F_R=F_1+F_2\).

Opposite directions

Subtract magnitudes: \(F_R=|F_1-F_2|\), direction of the larger force.

Dynamics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Same-Line Resultant Force

Problem

Two forces act on a cart: \(18\,\text{N}\) east and \(27\,\text{N}\) west. Find the resultant force.

1
Opposite directionsSubtract the smaller from the larger.
2
Magnitude\(F_R=27-18=9\,\text{N}\).
3
DirectionThe larger force is west.
4
AnswerResultant force = \(9\,\text{N west}\).
Dynamics - Idea 4

Adding Two Forces at Right Angle

When two forces act at right angles, the resultant is the diagonal of a right triangle.

Magnitude of resultant
\[F_R=\sqrt{F_1^2+F_2^2}\]
Dynamics - Idea 4

Finding the Direction of the Resultant

For perpendicular forces, the angle can be found using trigonometry.

Angle with horizontal force
\[\tan\theta=\frac{F_{\text{vertical}}}{F_{\text{horizontal}}}\]

Magnitude tells “how strong”

Use Pythagoras: \(F_R=\sqrt{F_x^2+F_y^2}\).

Angle tells “which direction”

Use tangent: \(\theta=\tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{F_y}{F_x}\right)\).

Calculator note: Make sure your calculator is in degree mode when the answer is expected in degrees.

Dynamics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Right-Angle Force Addition

Problem

A box is pulled by \(24\,\text{N}\) east and \(10\,\text{N}\) north. Find the resultant force and its direction.

1
Magnitude\(F_R=\sqrt{24^2+10^2}\).
2
Calculate\(F_R=\sqrt{576+100}=\sqrt{676}=26\,\text{N}\).
3
Angle\(\tan\theta=\frac{10}{24}\), so \(\theta\approx22.6^\circ\).
4
Answer\(26\,\text{N}\), \(22.6^\circ\) north of east.
Dynamics - Idea 5

Newton’s Second Law of Motion

Law

The acceleration of a body is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

Newton’s second law
\[\sum \vec F = m\vec a\]

\(\sum F\)

Net force, in newtons.

\(m\)

Mass, in kilograms.

\(a\)

Acceleration, in \(\text{m/s}^2\).

Dynamics - Idea 5

Solving for Acceleration

Most entrance questions ask you to find acceleration from mass and net force.

Acceleration form
\[a=\frac{\sum F}{m}\]

More net force

For the same mass, more net force means greater acceleration.

More mass

For the same net force, more mass means smaller acceleration.

Important: Use net force, not just one force, unless the problem says it is the only force acting in that direction.

Dynamics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Acceleration from Net Force

Problem

A \(6\,\text{kg}\) object has a net force of \(18\,\text{N}\) acting to the right. Find its acceleration.

1
Known values\(m=6\,\text{kg}\), \(F_{\text{net}}=18\,\text{N}\).
2
Formula\(a=\frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m}\).
3
Substitute\(a=\frac{18}{6}\).
4
Answer\(a=3\,\text{m/s}^2\) to the right.
Dynamics - Worked Example

Worked Example: Forces First, Then Acceleration

Problem

A \(4\,\text{kg}\) cart is pushed by \(35\,\text{N}\) to the right and \(11\,\text{N}\) to the left. Find its acceleration.

1
Find net force\(F_{\text{net}}=35-11=24\,\text{N}\) right.
2
Use Newton’s law\(a=\frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m}\).
3
Substitute\(a=\frac{24}{4}\).
4
Answer\(a=6\,\text{m/s}^2\) to the right.

Correct order: find resultant force first, then apply \(F=ma\).

Dynamics - Mini Quiz

Mini Quiz 2: Dynamics and Forces

Instructions

Show a force diagram mentally or on paper before calculating.

  1. Two forces of 14 N and 9 N act to the right. Find the resultant.
  2. Two forces of 40 N east and 25 N west act on a body. Find the resultant.
  3. Forces of 12 N north and 5 N east act at right angle. Find the magnitude of the resultant.
  4. A 10 kg body experiences a net force of 45 N. Find acceleration.
  5. A 3 kg object is pulled by 20 N right and 8 N left. Find acceleration and direction.
Dynamics - Solutions

Mini Quiz 2: Solutions

  1. Same direction: \(14+9=23\,\text{N}\) right.
  2. Opposite directions: \(40-25=15\,\text{N east}\).
  3. \(F_R=\sqrt{12^2+5^2}=\sqrt{169}=13\,\text{N}\).
  4. \(a=\frac{45}{10}=4.5\,\text{m/s}^2\).
  5. \(F_{\text{net}}=20-8=12\,\text{N}\) right. \(a=\frac{12}{3}=4\,\text{m/s}^2\) right.
Topic 2 - Electricity

Electric Current and Voltage

Electricity questions usually ask you to track charge flow, voltage distribution, current distribution, resistance, energy, and power.

Current

How much charge passes a point each second.

Voltage

Energy transferred per unit charge between two points.

Electricity - Idea 1

Electric Current

Definition

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

Current formula
\[I=\frac{Q}{t}\]

\(I\)

Current in amperes, A.

\(Q\)

Charge in coulombs, C.

\(t\)

Time in seconds, s.

Electricity - Idea 1

Current Needs a Closed Circuit

Closed circuit

There is a complete conducting path. Current can flow.

Open circuit

The path is broken. Current cannot flow.

Electricity - Worked Example

Worked Example: Calculating Current

Problem

A charge of \(90\,\text{C}\) passes through a wire in \(30\,\text{s}\). Find the current.

1
Known values\(Q=90\,\text{C}\), \(t=30\,\text{s}\).
2
Formula\(I=\frac{Q}{t}\).
3
Substitute\(I=\frac{90}{30}\).
4
Answer\(I=3\,\text{A}\).
Electricity - Idea 2

Voltage

Definition

Voltage, or potential difference, is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points in a circuit.

Voltage formula
\[V=\frac{E}{Q}\]

\(V\)

Voltage in volts, V.

\(E\)

Energy in joules, J.

\(Q\)

Charge in coulombs, C.

Electricity - Idea 2

What Voltage Means Physically

Voltage does not mean “amount of current.” Voltage is more like electrical push or energy per charge.

Battery/source

A source provides energy to charges. A 9 V battery gives 9 J of energy to each coulomb of charge.

Component/load

A resistor, lamp, or motor uses electrical energy. Voltage across it measures energy used per coulomb.

Important distinction: Current is charge per second. Voltage is energy per charge.

Electricity - Worked Example

Worked Example: Calculating Voltage

Problem

A component transfers \(240\,\text{J}\) of energy when \(20\,\text{C}\) of charge passes through it. Find the voltage across the component.

1
Known values\(E=240\,\text{J}\), \(Q=20\,\text{C}\).
2
Formula\(V=\frac{E}{Q}\).
3
Substitute\(V=\frac{240}{20}\).
4
Answer\(V=12\,\text{V}\).
Electricity - Idea 3

Current in Series and Parallel Circuits

Series circuit

There is only one path, so the same current passes through all components.


\[I_1=I_2=I_3\]

Parallel circuit

Current splits between branches and recombines after them.


\[I_{\text{total}}=I_1+I_2+\cdots\]

Electricity - Idea 3

Voltage in Series and Parallel Circuits

Series circuit

The source voltage is shared between components.


\[V_{\text{source}}=V_1+V_2+\cdots\]

Parallel circuit

Each branch has the same voltage as the source.


\[V_1=V_2=V_{\text{source}}\]

Memory trick: Series → current same, voltage divides. Parallel → voltage same, current divides.

Electricity - Worked Example

Worked Example: Current and Voltage Laws

Problem

Two lamps are connected in series to a 12 V battery. The voltage across lamp 1 is 5 V. The current in lamp 1 is 0.4 A. Find the voltage across lamp 2 and the current in lamp 2.

1
Series voltage\(V_{\text{source}}=V_1+V_2\).
2
Find missing voltage\(12=5+V_2\), so \(V_2=7\,\text{V}\).
3
Series currentThe current is the same everywhere in a series circuit.
4
Answer\(V_2=7\,\text{V}\), \(I_2=0.4\,\text{A}\).
Electricity - Mini Quiz

Mini Quiz 3: Current and Voltage

Instructions

Focus on definitions and circuit laws before calculating.

  1. A charge of 150 C passes in 50 s. Find current.
  2. A device uses 600 J when 50 C passes through it. Find voltage.
  3. Three resistors are in series. If the current through the first is 0.2 A, what is the current through the third?
  4. Two components are in series across 18 V. One has 7 V across it. Find the voltage across the other.
  5. Two branches are in parallel across a 9 V battery. What voltage is across each branch?
Electricity - Solutions

Mini Quiz 3: Solutions

  1. \(I=\frac{Q}{t}=\frac{150}{50}=3\,\text{A}\).
  2. \(V=\frac{E}{Q}=\frac{600}{50}=12\,\text{V}\).
  3. Same current in series: \(0.2\,\text{A}\).
  4. \(18-7=11\,\text{V}\).
  5. Parallel voltage is the same as source: \(9\,\text{V}\) across each branch.
Topic 2 - Electricity

Resistance and Ohm’s Law

Resistance tells how strongly a component opposes electric current. It links voltage and current through Ohm’s law.

Concept

Greater resistance makes current smaller for the same voltage.

Main formula

\(V=IR\)

Resistance - Idea 1

Electrical Resistance

Definition

Electrical resistance is the opposition that a material or component offers to the flow of electric current.

Resistance unit
\[R \text{ is measured in ohms } (\Omega)\]

Low resistance

Current can flow more easily.

High resistance

Current flow is more difficult.

Resistance - Idea 1

How Resistance Affects Current

Think of current like water flow and resistance like a narrow pipe. A narrower path makes flow harder.

This analogy is useful, but not perfect. In calculations, use Ohm’s law.

Resistance - Idea 2

Ohm’s Law

Law

For an ohmic resistor at constant temperature, the voltage across it is directly proportional to the current through it.

Ohm’s law
\[V=IR\]

Find voltage

\(V=IR\)

Find current

\(I=\frac{V}{R}\)

Find resistance

\(R=\frac{V}{I}\)

Resistance - Idea 2

Voltage-Current Graph for an Ohmic Resistor

For an ohmic resistor, the graph of voltage against current is a straight line through the origin.

A steeper line means larger resistance because more voltage is needed for the same current.

Resistance - Worked Example

Worked Example: Ohm’s Law

Problem

A \(6\,\Omega\) resistor is connected to a \(12\,\text{V}\) supply. Find the current.

1
Known values\(R=6\,\Omega\), \(V=12\,\text{V}\).
2
Rearrange\(I=\frac{V}{R}\).
3
Substitute\(I=\frac{12}{6}\).
4
Answer\(I=2\,\text{A}\).
Resistance - Idea 3

Grouping Resistors in Series

Series connection

Resistors are in series when they are connected one after another in a single path.

Equivalent resistance in series
\[R_{\text{eq}}=R_1+R_2+R_3+\cdots\]
Resistance - Worked Example

Worked Example: Series Resistors

Problem

Three resistors \(4\,\Omega\), \(7\,\Omega\), and \(9\,\Omega\) are connected in series to a \(40\,\text{V}\) source. Find the equivalent resistance and total current.

1
Series resistance\(R_{\text{eq}}=4+7+9=20\,\Omega\).
2
Use Ohm’s law\(I=\frac{V}{R_{\text{eq}}}\).
3
Substitute\(I=\frac{40}{20}\).
4
Answer\(R_{\text{eq}}=20\,\Omega\), \(I=2\,\text{A}\).
Resistance - Idea 4

Grouping Resistors in Parallel

Parallel connection

Resistors are in parallel when their ends are connected to the same two points, giving multiple paths for current.

Equivalent resistance in parallel
\[\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}}=\frac{1}{R_1}+\frac{1}{R_2}+\frac{1}{R_3}+\cdots\]
Resistance - Worked Example

Worked Example: Parallel Resistors

Problem

Two resistors \(6\,\Omega\) and \(3\,\Omega\) are connected in parallel. Find the equivalent resistance.

1
Formula\(\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}}=\frac{1}{6}+\frac{1}{3}\).
2
Common denominator\(\frac{1}{6}+\frac{2}{6}=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2}\).
3
Invert\(\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}}=\frac{1}{2}\), so \(R_{\text{eq}}=2\,\Omega\).
4
CheckParallel resistance is less than the smallest branch resistor. \(2\Omega<3\Omega\), so it makes sense.
Resistance - Idea 5

Mixed Series-Parallel Circuits

A mixed circuit contains both series and parallel parts. Solve it by simplifying one part at a time.

1
Identify groupsFind resistors clearly in series or clearly in parallel.
2
Simplify one groupReplace that group by its equivalent resistance.
3
RepeatContinue until one total resistance remains.
4
Apply Ohm’s lawUse \(V=IR\) for total values or individual branches.

Warning: Do not add all resistors directly unless they are all in series.

Resistance - Worked Example

Worked Example: Mixed Resistor Circuit

Problem

A \(4\,\Omega\) resistor is in series with a parallel combination of \(6\,\Omega\) and \(3\,\Omega\). The circuit is connected to \(18\,\text{V}\). Find total resistance and total current.

1
Parallel part\(\frac{1}{R_p}=\frac{1}{6}+\frac{1}{3}=\frac{1}{2}\), so \(R_p=2\,\Omega\).
2
Series total\(R_{\text{eq}}=4+2=6\,\Omega\).
3
Total current\(I=\frac{V}{R}=\frac{18}{6}\).
4
Answer\(R_{\text{eq}}=6\,\Omega\), \(I=3\,\text{A}\).
Resistance - Mini Quiz

Mini Quiz 4: Resistance and Resistor Grouping

Instructions

Decide first: series, parallel, or mixed?

  1. A resistor has \(10\,\text{V}\) across it and \(2\,\text{A}\) through it. Find \(R\).
  2. A \(12\,\Omega\) resistor is connected to \(6\,\text{V}\). Find current.
  3. Find equivalent resistance of \(5\,\Omega\), \(8\,\Omega\), and \(2\,\Omega\) in series.
  4. Find equivalent resistance of \(4\,\Omega\) and \(12\,\Omega\) in parallel.
  5. A \(2\,\Omega\) resistor is in series with two parallel resistors \(6\,\Omega\) and \(3\,\Omega\). Find total resistance.
Resistance - Solutions

Mini Quiz 4: Solutions

  1. \(R=\frac{V}{I}=\frac{10}{2}=5\,\Omega\).
  2. \(I=\frac{V}{R}=\frac{6}{12}=0.5\,\text{A}\).
  3. Series: \(R_{\text{eq}}=5+8+2=15\,\Omega\).
  4. \(\frac{1}{R}=\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{12}=\frac{4}{12}=\frac{1}{3}\), so \(R=3\,\Omega\).
  5. Parallel part: \(6\parallel3=2\,\Omega\). Total: \(2+2=4\,\Omega\).
Topic 2 - Electricity

Electrical Energy and Power

Power tells how fast electrical energy is transferred. Energy tells the total amount transferred over time.

Power

Rate of energy transfer.

Energy

Total transferred energy.

Energy and Power - Idea 1

Electrical Power

Definition

Electrical power is the rate at which electrical energy is transferred or converted by a device.

Power formula
\[P=VI\]

\(P\)

Power in watts, W.

\(V\)

Voltage in volts, V.

\(I\)

Current in amperes, A.

Energy and Power - Idea 1

Power Formulas Using Resistance

By combining \(P=VI\) with Ohm’s law \(V=IR\), we get two other useful formulas.

Using current and resistance
\[P=I^2R\]
Using voltage and resistance
\[P=\frac{V^2}{R}\]

Use the formula that matches the given data. If the question gives \(V\) and \(I\), use \(P=VI\). If it gives \(I\) and \(R\), use \(P=I^2R\).

Energy and Power - Worked Example

Worked Example: Calculating Power

Problem

A lamp has \(12\,\text{V}\) across it and carries a current of \(0.5\,\text{A}\). Find its power.

1
Known values\(V=12\,\text{V}\), \(I=0.5\,\text{A}\).
2
Formula\(P=VI\).
3
Substitute\(P=12\times0.5\).
4
Answer\(P=6\,\text{W}\).
Energy and Power - Idea 2

Electrical Energy

Definition

Electrical energy is the total energy transferred by an electrical device during a time interval.

Energy formula
\[E=Pt\]

\(E\)

Energy in joules, J.

\(P\)

Power in watts, W.

\(t\)

Time in seconds, s.

Energy and Power - Idea 2

Energy Formula Variations

Since \(P=VI\), electrical energy can also be written using voltage and current.

Using power
\[E=Pt\]
Using voltage and current
\[E=VIt\]

If \(P\) is in watts and \(t\) is in seconds, energy is in joules. If power is in kilowatts and time is in hours, energy is in kilowatt-hours.

Energy and Power - Worked Example

Worked Example: Calculating Electrical Energy

Problem

A \(60\,\text{W}\) lamp operates for \(5\,\text{min}\). Find the electrical energy used in joules.

1
Convert time\(5\,\text{min}=300\,\text{s}\).
2
Formula\(E=Pt\).
3
Substitute\(E=60\times300\).
4
Answer\(E=18{,}000\,\text{J}\).
Energy and Power - Idea 3

Power vs Energy: Do Not Mix Them Up

Power

How fast energy is transferred.


Unit: watt \((\text{W})\)

Formula: \(P=\frac{E}{t}\)

Energy

Total amount transferred.


Unit: joule \((\text{J})\)

Formula: \(E=Pt\)

A high-power device does not always use more energy. It depends on how long it operates.

Energy and Power - Worked Example

Worked Example: Which Device Uses More Energy?

Problem

Device A has power \(100\,\text{W}\) and runs for \(20\,\text{s}\). Device B has power \(40\,\text{W}\) and runs for \(90\,\text{s}\). Which uses more energy?

1
Device A\(E_A=Pt=100\times20=2000\,\text{J}\).
2
Device B\(E_B=40\times90=3600\,\text{J}\).
3
Compare\(3600\,\text{J} > 2000\,\text{J}\).
4
ConclusionDevice B uses more energy, even though its power is lower.

Foundation

Scalar vs Vector Quantities

Many mistakes in mechanics come from treating vector quantities as if they were scalars.

Scalar

Has magnitude only.


Examples: distance, speed, time, mass, energy, power.

Vector

Has magnitude and direction.


Examples: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force.

Exam rule: If direction matters, draw an arrow and use signs such as positive/negative, east/west, or up/down.

Kinematics - Strategy

How to Choose the Correct Kinematics Formula

1
Path length?If the question gives total path traveled, think distance and average speed.
2
Change in position?If start and finish positions matter, think displacement and average velocity.
3
Change in velocity?If velocity changes with time, use acceleration.
4
Graph?If it is an x-t graph, velocity is the slope.

Before calculating, write the target quantity: distance, displacement, speed, velocity, or acceleration.

Kinematics - Graphs

Piecewise Displacement-Time Graphs

A graph can have different sections. Each straight section has its own velocity.

For each part, calculate slope separately. Do not use the whole graph unless the question asks for average velocity over the whole interval.

Electricity - Measurement

How Current and Voltage Are Measured

Ammeter

Measures current. It is connected in series because it must count the charge passing through the component.

Voltmeter

Measures voltage. It is connected in parallel because it compares energy per charge between two points.

This reinforces the definitions: current is flow through a component; voltage is difference across a component.

Electricity - Strategy

Series vs Parallel: Strategy Table

Series

  • One path.
  • Same current everywhere.
  • Voltages add.
  • Resistances add directly.

Parallel

  • Multiple paths.
  • Same voltage across branches.
  • Currents add.
  • Use reciprocal formula for resistance.
Resistance - Worked Example

Worked Example: Current in Parallel Branches

Problem

Two resistors, \(6\,\Omega\) and \(12\,\Omega\), are connected in parallel across a \(24\,\text{V}\) source. Find the current in each branch and the total current.

1
Parallel voltageEach branch has \(24\,\text{V}\).
2
Branch 1\(I_1=\frac{24}{6}=4\,\text{A}\).
3
Branch 2\(I_2=\frac{24}{12}=2\,\text{A}\).
4
Total\(I_{\text{total}}=4+2=6\,\text{A}\).
Energy and Power - Units

Energy Units: Joules and Kilowatt-Hours

Joule

Used in physics calculations when power is in watts and time is in seconds.


\(E=Pt\)

Kilowatt-hour

Used for electricity bills when power is in kilowatts and time is in hours.


\(1\,\text{kWh}=3.6\times10^6\,\text{J}\)

Do not mix watts with hours if the answer is required in joules. Convert time to seconds first.

Energy and Power - Worked Example

Worked Example: Power in a Resistor

Problem

A \(10\,\Omega\) resistor is connected to a \(20\,\text{V}\) source. Find the current and power dissipated.

1
Current\(I=\frac{V}{R}=\frac{20}{10}=2\,\text{A}\).
2
Power method 1\(P=VI=20\times2=40\,\text{W}\).
3
Power method 2\(P=\frac{V^2}{R}=\frac{20^2}{10}=40\,\text{W}\).
4
AnswerCurrent \(=2\,\text{A}\), power \(=40\,\text{W}\).

Summary

Formula Summary: Mechanics

Distance and speed

  • \(v=\frac{d}{t}\)
  • \(d=vt\)
  • \(t=\frac{d}{v}\)

Displacement and velocity

  • \(\vec v=\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\)
  • Slope of x-t graph = velocity

Acceleration

  • \(a=\frac{v_f-v_i}{t}\)
  • \(v_f=v_i+at\)

Forces

  • Same line: add/subtract
  • Right angle: \(F_R=\sqrt{F_1^2+F_2^2}\)

Newton’s law

  • \(\sum F=ma\)
  • \(a=\frac{\sum F}{m}\)

Units

  • m, s, m/s, m/s²
  • N = kg·m/s²
Summary

Formula Summary: Electricity

Current

  • \(I=\frac{Q}{t}\)
  • Unit: ampere, A

Voltage

  • \(V=\frac{E}{Q}\)
  • Unit: volt, V

Resistance

  • \(V=IR\)
  • Unit: ohm, \(\Omega\)

Grouping

  • Series: \(R=R_1+R_2+\cdots\)
  • Parallel: \(\frac{1}{R}=\frac{1}{R_1}+\frac{1}{R_2}+\cdots\)

Power

  • \(P=VI\)
  • \(P=I^2R\)
  • \(P=\frac{V^2}{R}\)

Energy

  • \(E=Pt\)
  • \(E=VIt\)
Exam Strategy

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mechanics mistakes

  • Confusing distance with displacement.
  • Confusing speed with velocity.
  • Using one force instead of net force.
  • Forgetting direction when adding vectors.

Electricity mistakes

  • Thinking current and voltage are the same.
  • Using series rules for parallel circuits.
  • Adding parallel resistors directly.
  • Using minutes or hours without converting when energy must be in joules.

Final check: Does your answer have the correct unit and a reasonable size?

Final Test Solutions

Solution: Question 1

  1. Distance \(=60+25=85\,\text{m}\).
  2. Displacement \(=60-25=35\,\text{m east}\).
  3. Total time \(=30+10=40\,\text{s}\). Average speed \(=\frac{85}{40}=2.125\,\text{m/s}\).
  4. Average velocity \(=\frac{35}{40}=0.875\,\text{m/s east}\).
  5. Speed uses total distance; velocity uses displacement. Backtracking increases distance but reduces displacement.
Final Test Solutions

Solution: Question 2

  1. \(a=\frac{23-5}{6}=3\,\text{m/s}^2\).
  2. After another \(4\,\text{s}\): \(v_f=23+(3)(4)=35\,\text{m/s}\).
  3. Negative slope means velocity is negative: the object moves in the negative direction.
  4. \(v=\frac{-6-18}{8-2}=\frac{-24}{6}=-4\,\text{m/s}\).
Final Test Solutions

Solution: Question 3

  1. Draw two arrows right labeled 30 N and 8 N, and one arrow left labeled 12 N.
  2. Net force \(=30+8-12=26\,\text{N right}\).
  3. \(a=\frac{F}{m}=\frac{26}{5}=5.2\,\text{m/s}^2\) right.
  4. For perpendicular forces: \(F_R=\sqrt{24^2+7^2}=\sqrt{625}=25\,\text{N}\).
  5. Measured north of east: \(\tan\theta=\frac{24}{7}\), so \(\theta\approx73.7^\circ\) north of east.
Final Test Solutions

Solution: Question 4

  1. Parallel part: \(\frac{1}{R_p}=\frac{1}{6}+\frac{1}{6}=\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}\), so \(R_p=3\,\Omega\).
  2. Total resistance: \(R_{\text{eq}}=3+3=6\,\Omega\).
  3. Total current: \(I=\frac{12}{6}=2\,\text{A}\).
  4. Voltage across series \(3\,\Omega\): \(V=IR=2\times3=6\,\text{V}\).
  5. Remaining voltage across parallel branch: \(12-6=6\,\text{V}\).
Final Test Solutions

Solution: Question 5

  1. \(P=VI=9\times0.8=7.2\,\text{W}\).
  2. \(5\,\text{min}=300\,\text{s}\). \(E=Pt=7.2\times300=2160\,\text{J}\).
  3. \(R=\frac{V}{I}=\frac{9}{0.8}=11.25\,\Omega\).
  4. Other device: \(P=\frac{3000}{200}=15\,\text{W}\), so the other device has greater power.
  5. Power is how quickly energy is used; energy is the total used over time. The motor has lower power but still uses energy because it runs for several minutes.
